Design Intelligence

What shapes a building on this site - and why every floor changes the equation

StructureCore Arrangement

The core - lifts, stairs, risers, WCs - is the single largest non-lettable element. At low-rise (≤10F), a compact core with a single staircase and 2 lifts occupies ~18% of the plate. By 14F you need 4–6 lifts and CIBSE Guide D round-trip targets start biting. At 22F, a sky lobby at L12 with 8 lifts, 2 firefighter lifts, and 2 staircases pushes core to 25%+ of plate.

On this site, the core is positioned NE to protect south and west lease spans - the premium faces. Every m² of core is a m² you can't let. At £800/m² ERV on a 5.25% yield, each extra m² of core costs ~£15,200 in capital value.

On this site: core grows from ~280m² (Low) to ~430m² (High) - a 150m² increase that erases ~£2.3M in GDV before you've added a single constraint.

DaylightRights of Light

Rights of light in England are a legal right (Prescription Act 1832) - not just a planning matter. An affected neighbour can seek an injunction to demolish if their light is materially reduced. BRE 209 assesses three metrics: Vertical Sky Component (target: 27%), Average Daylight Factor, and Annual Probable Sunlight Hours.

At 8F this site has minimal daylight impact - matching the existing street section. At 10–12F, south-facing residential on Southwark Street upper floors requires assessment. A BRE daylight/sunlight report is standard from Stage 2. Rights of light negotiation budget: £200K–£600K depending on affected windows.

Critical receptors: Tate Modern (220m W, Grade II*) and residential amenity in Southwark Street residential upper floors. Neither constrains massing at 8–12F. Heritage setting assessment standard but not blocking.

👁️LVMF Protected Views

The London View Management Framework (LVMF 2012) protects 27 strategic views. No protected views cross this Bankside CAZ site at 8–12F. The site does not sit within any LVMF assessment corridor at the proposed heights - a material advantage over EC2/EC3 comparables.

At 10–14F, this site sits well below the threshold and a precautionary review suffices. At 22F, the building enters the background consultation zone - a full 3D verified view assessment is required, submitted to the GLA. This adds 6–12 weeks to the planning timeline and can result in a height cap if the proposal breaches the viewing corridor.

👁️ The Cluster's existing skyline (22 Bishopsgate at 278m, Cheesegrater at 224m) provides some cover - but at 92m on a site this far south in the Cluster, the LVMF geometry is tight.

🔥Building Safety Act - Compliance Cascade

The BSA 2022 creates hard thresholds. Above 18m: the building is registered, an Accountable Person is appointed, and a Safety Case File is maintained for the building's life. Above 50m: it becomes a Higher Risk Building - mandatory second staircase (simultaneous evacuation), firefighter lifts, gateway approvals at planning, pre-construction, and pre-occupation.

The 2nd staircase costs ~£1.4M and consumes ~40m² per floor of lettable area. Over 8 tower floors (Base scenario), that's 320m² of lost NIA - roughly £4.9M in capital value.

🔥 The 50m threshold is the single most significant cost trigger on this site. It's why the return curve peaks at ~12–14F rather than continuing to rise.

📈Construction Cost Escalation

Construction costs don't scale linearly. Below 10F: standard RC frame, tower crane, conventional formwork - £4,100/m². At 14F: climbing formwork, deeper piles, more complex facade logistics - £5,000/m². At 22F: jump-form systems, higher-grade concrete (C60+), complex curtain walling at height, wind-design steel - £6,250/m².

The programme extension matters as much as the build cost. A 10F building takes ~2.5 years. A 22F tower takes 4 years. That extra 18 months of development finance at 7.5% on a £35M construction cost adds ~£2.0M in interest alone - money that comes straight off the developer's margin.

📈 £4,100 → £6,250/m² is a 52% cost premium. But the GDV per m² NIA rises only ~13% (£750 → £850 ERV). That divergence is why the margin curve bends.

EnvironmentWind Microclimate

Tall buildings create downdraught (Venturi effect) and corner acceleration. CIBSE AM14 defines comfort criteria for pedestrians: sitting, standing, walking. Above ~60m, a wind microclimate study is expected by City planners - above 80m it's mandatory.

This site sits 200m south of 22 Bishopsgate (278m). The prevailing SW wind creates a pressure differential on the east face. At 22F, mitigation is likely required: podium canopies, corner chamfers, tree planting in the public realm. Budget £200K–£400K for the study and mitigation design.

The Low scenario avoids this entirely. Base triggers a desktop assessment. High requires a full boundary-layer wind tunnel or CFD study - 10–14 week programme.

StructureStructural Systems

Minimum 7m clear lease span on at least 2 faces of core to facade - the threshold for Grade A lettability. Below this, floor plates feel constrained and rents discount. Above 10m, column-free spans require transfer beams that push floor-to-floor height, increasing total building height without adding floors.

At 10F (Low): simple RC flat slab, 250mm slab, 4.2m F-F height, comfortable 11m clear spans south and west. At 22F (High): the core expands, the tower footprint shrinks to 40% coverage - clear spans compress to ~7m, which is the minimum. Any further height and the plate starts failing the lease span test.

The structural grid also influences facade design. Column spacing at 7.5m centres means the facade module is fixed - structural moves cascade into architectural expression.

💷Programme & Finance Compounding

Development finance is typically interest-only during construction, drawn against the facility as the build progresses. On a £35M build with 60% LTC at 7.5%, the effective annual interest (accounting for staged drawdown) is ~£780K. But finance cost compounds with programme extension.

A 6-month delay - not unusual in tower construction - adds ~£650K in interest plus holding costs. This is why programme risk is the most underestimated variable in tall building feasibility: the margin looks healthy at appraisal, but a single winter of bad weather or a cladding procurement delay can erode 2–3% of profit on cost.

💷 Low scenario: £1.7M finance cost. High scenario: £5.2M. The £3.5M difference is pure time-cost - building the same NIA, just taking longer to do it.

📋Planning Policy - City Local Plan

The London Borough of Southwark's Core Strategy designates this area within the Bankside Central Activities Zone. Policy SP1 supports office intensification. No tall buildings policy applies at 8–12F - schemes above surroundings require design justification in the D&A Statement but do not trigger a separate tall buildings assessment.

However, "encouraged" ≠ "automatic". Key requirements: exceptional architectural quality, active ground floor uses, public realm contribution, and demonstration that the height is the result of a design-led process (not just commercial optimisation). The higher the building, the higher the design bar.

📋 At 10F, you're below the Cluster's radar - standard planning. At 22F, expect a Planning Performance Agreement (PPA), design review panel, and potentially a call-in by the Secretary of State.

HeritageSetting of Listed Buildings & WHS

Heritage impact operates under NPPF §§199–208. At this Bankside CAZ location, the heritage profile is significantly cleaner than EC2/EC3 comparables. The nearest Grade II* asset is Tate Modern at 220m west - its setting is predominantly defined by the Millennium Bridge axis and the Thames frontage, not Southwark Street. A visual impact assessment is standard procedure but is unlikely to constrain height at 8–12F.

Borough Market lies 400m SE within the Borough Market Conservation Area, but this designation does not extend to 24 Southwark Street. No LVMF protected views cross this site at the proposed heights. The site is not within any Conservation Area and has no Article 4 Directions that would restrict the proposed use class.

What this means in practice: A Heritage Impact Assessment (HIA) is standard procedure and should be prepared from RIBA Stage 2. The assessment burden is materially lower than an EC3 scheme - Historic England is unlikely to object and the design quality bar for heritage approval is moderate. A D&A Statement addressing Tate Modern setting and Southwark streetscape will be sufficient for all three scenarios.

On this site: the south and east faces are heritage-constrained at every scenario height. The massing setbacks shown (south face recedes at all scenarios, east steps back at Base and High) are the minimum response. A pre-app heritage scoping meeting with Historic England before any massing is fixed is strongly recommended.

MassingSetback Logic

Setbacks on tall buildings serve multiple purposes simultaneously: planning context (responding to neighbouring buildings and conservation areas), daylight compliance, LVMF sightline management, wind mitigation, and structural expression.

On this site, the primary setback consideration faces west - responding to Tate Modern sightlines at 220m. South face holds to the Southwark Street building line. North and east faces are unconstrained. The setback calculus is significantly simpler than an EC3 equivalent - no heritage sightlines require recession and no mandatory setback is needed at 8–12F.

Current coverage factors: Low 65%, Base 55%, High 40% of site area. These are calibrated feasibility estimates. A pre-application submission would derive exact setback depths from BRE 209 shadow path analysis, right-to-light 50/50 line geometry, and LVMF corridor modelling.

📐 Principle: the primary receptor face recedes, all others hold unless there's a named planning or environmental reason. Steps need a structural or contextual hook - they're not arbitrary.
