Design Intelligence

What shapes a building on this site — and why every floor changes the equation

StructureCore Arrangement

On a campus site this size, the core configuration is driven by tower floor plate area rather than total site coverage. At 10F (Low), a compact core with a single staircase and 2 lifts occupies ~18% of a 2,250 m² plate — well within Grade A standards. By 18F (Base), you need 4–6 lifts, a mandatory 2nd staircase (BSA >50m), and the core grows to ~22% of plate. At 30F (High), a sky lobby at L15 with 8 lifts, 2 firefighter lifts, and 2 staircases pushes core to 24%+ of a 1,620 m² plate.

On this site, the core should be positioned to protect south-facing lease spans — the premium orientation overlooking the Barbican lakeside and gardens. Every m² of core is a m² you can't let. At £700/m² ERV on a 5.3% yield, each extra m² of core costs ~£13,200 in capital value.

On this site: core grows from ~400m² (Low) to ~520m² (High) — a 120m² increase across a larger plate, but the NIA impact is compounded by 27 tower floors at High vs 6 at Low.

DaylightRights of Light — Barbican Residents

The critical daylight receptors here are fundamentally different from the Eastern Cluster. The Barbican Estate — 1,750 flats in Cromwell, Shakespeare, and Lauderdale towers plus the terrace blocks — sits immediately north. These are residential windows with full rights of light protection under the Prescription Act 1832.

At 10F (40m), the impact is minimal — the site sits below the Barbican podium datum. At 18F (72m), south-facing Barbican flats begin to lose Vertical Sky Component. At 30F (123m), a full BRE 209 assessment is mandatory and rights-of-light compensation is highly likely — budget £1M–£3M given the number of affected residential windows.

Critical receptors: south-facing Barbican residential windows (N), Willoughby House terrace (NE), and the Barbican Centre itself (NW). The north face is the constrained massing face on this site.

RegulationBuilding Safety Act

The BSA 2022 creates hard thresholds. Above 18m: the building is registered, an Accountable Person is appointed. Above 50m: Higher Risk Building — mandatory 2nd staircase (simultaneous evacuation), firefighter lifts, BSR gateway approvals at planning, pre-construction, and pre-occupation.

All three scenarios exceed 18m. The Low scenario (40m) stays below 50m — single staircase permissible, which is a significant cost and efficiency advantage. The Base scenario (72m) triggers the full HRB regime. The High scenario (123m) adds aviation lighting and specialist fire engineering.

The 50m threshold is the single most significant cost trigger. The 2nd staircase costs ~£1.6M and consumes ~40m² per upper floor. Over 13 tower floors (Base), that's 520m² of lost NIA — roughly £6.8M in capital value.

CostConstruction Cost Escalation

Construction costs don't scale linearly. At 10F (Low): standard RC frame, tower crane, conventional formwork — £4,200/m². At 18F (Base): climbing formwork, deeper piles, more complex facade logistics — £5,200/m². At 30F (High): jump-form systems, higher-grade concrete (C60+), complex curtain walling at height — £6,500/m².

The programme extension is critical on a campus this size. Demolition alone takes 12–18 months (asbestos surveys, Barbican vibration monitoring). A 10F scheme takes ~2.5 years post-demo. A 32F tower takes 5 years. The extra 30 months of development finance on a £200M+ construction cost adds ~£12M in interest.

£4,200 → £6,500/m² is a 55% cost premium. But the ERV per m² NIA rises only ~23% (£620 → £760). That divergence is why the margin curve bends hard above ~20F.

EnvironmentWind Microclimate

This site is exposed to the prevailing SW wind along the Moor Lane corridor. The Barbican podium-and-tower typology creates complex turbulence patterns at ground level. CIBSE AM14 defines comfort criteria for pedestrians: sitting, standing, walking.

At 10F (40m), the scheme sits within the surrounding built context — wind impact is modest. At 18F (72m), downdraught on the south elevation (Silk Street) becomes a concern for pedestrian comfort. At 30F (123m), full wind tunnel or CFD study is mandatory — the building would be the tallest structure in its immediate context and the Barbican's open lakeside creates a fetch that accelerates ground-level winds.

Mitigation at the High scenario: podium canopies on Silk Street, corner chamfers above L20, and tree planting along Moor Lane. Budget £300K–£500K for study and mitigation design.

StructureStructural Systems & Lease Spans

Minimum 7m clear lease span on at least 2 faces of core to facade — the threshold for Grade A lettability. The tower plate at Low (2,250 m², 25% coverage) delivers comfortable 10m+ clear spans on all faces. At Base (2,000 m², 22% coverage), clear spans are ~9m — still strong. At High (1,620 m², 18% coverage), the slender plate compresses spans to ~7.5m — viable but tight.

The podium floors (4,500 m² at 50% coverage) are generous at all scenarios — clear spans of 12m+ with a regular structural grid. The campus arrangement allows natural light from multiple facades, which is a lettability advantage over deep single-building plates.

The structural transfer from podium to tower at L4/L5 is a key design move. Column offsets require transfer beams that add cost (~£1.5M) and programme (4–6 weeks).

FinanceProgramme & Finance Compounding

Development finance is typically interest-only during construction. On a £160M build (Base) with 60% LTC at 7.5%, the effective annual interest (accounting for staged drawdown) is ~£3.6M. But finance cost compounds with programme extension.

A 6-month delay on a campus demolition and rebuild — not unusual when working adjacent to a listed estate with vibration restrictions — adds ~£1.8M in interest plus holding costs. The Barbican Estate management company will impose strict construction management conditions: limited working hours, vibration monitoring, dust suppression. These add ~10% to programme duration.

Low scenario: ~£3.5M finance cost. High scenario: ~£16M. The £12.5M difference is time-cost — a 5-year programme vs 2.5 years.

PlanningPlanning Policy — NOT Eastern Cluster

This site sits outside the Eastern Cluster Policy Area. There is no presumption in favour of tall buildings here. The City of London Local Plan 2036 requires proposals for tall buildings outside designated clusters to demonstrate that the height is justified by the site's specific circumstances — not simply by commercial optimisation.

The relevant precedent is 21 Moorfields (81m, 2022, Land Securities) — the most recent tall building approved in this immediate area. Its planning committee report specifically addressed the heritage impact on the Barbican Estate and was approved on the basis of design quality, public realm contribution, and the specific opportunity presented by the Crossrail station box.

At 10F (40m), standard planning with GLA referral. At 18F (72m), expect PPA + design review panel. At 30F (123m), expect Secretary of State call-in risk and a 24-month+ planning timeline.

HeritageSetting of the Barbican Estate & St Giles Cripplegate

Heritage impact operates under NPPF §§199–208. A finding of harm to the setting of a Grade I or Grade II* listed building is a statutory bar to approval unless demonstrably outweighed by public benefit. "Setting" includes any land from which the heritage asset can be seen and experienced — and the Barbican Estate is experienced as a complete designed composition.

Three assets create overlapping setting zones: the Barbican Estate (Grade II*) immediately north — a brutalist masterpiece by Chamberlin, Powell & Bon, listed in its entirety including the lakeside, podium walkways, and tower-and-terrace composition. St Giles Cripplegate (Grade I) 80m north — a medieval parish church whose relationship with the Barbican lakeside is specifically noted in the conservation area appraisal. And the London Wall / Cripplegate Roman Fort — a scheduled ancient monument whose archaeological interest extends across the site perimeter.

The Barbican is a special case in heritage terms. Unlike a single listed building where the setting assessment is relatively bounded, the Barbican is a designed landscape — the towers, terraces, lake, and gardens form an intentional composition. A new tower visible from within this composition affects the setting not of one asset but of the entire ensemble. Historic England has consistently taken a protective stance on proposals affecting the Barbican setting.

What this means in practice: A Heritage Impact Assessment (HIA) must address the composite setting, not just individual sightlines. The north face of any scheme is the heritage-critical elevation. A scheme that actively responds to the Barbican's architectural language — material palette, horizontal datum lines, visual permeability at podium level — will perform better than one that contrasts with it. Pre-application engagement with Historic England is essential before any massing is fixed.

On this site: the north face is heritage-constrained at every scenario height. Tower setback from the Barbican boundary should increase with height. The relationship between any new tower and the existing Barbican towers (Cromwell, Shakespeare, Lauderdale at 123m) is the critical visual test — a scheme at 123m (High) would read as a peer to these towers, which is both the opportunity and the risk.

MassingSetback Logic

On this site, the primary setback faces north — responding to the Barbican Estate. The podium should hold the Silk Street building line (south) to maintain the campus character and street enclosure. The tower should step back from the north edge as it rises, increasing the gap between the new massing and the Barbican terrace blocks.

Tower coverage: Low 25%, Base 22%, High 18% of site area. The reduction in coverage at height is not just aesthetic — it responds directly to daylight impact on Barbican residential windows and reduces wind acceleration at the Barbican podium level. These are calibrated feasibility estimates. A pre-application submission would derive exact setback depths from BRE 209 shadow path analysis and heritage setting assessment geometry.

Principle: the primary receptor face (north/Barbican) recedes, all others hold unless there's a named reason. The south face (Silk Street) holds to maintain the street wall. East and west faces hold — Moor Lane and Fore Street are commercial corridors.
