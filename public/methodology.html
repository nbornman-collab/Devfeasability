Scoring Methodology

How D/A scores
development sites

Every site in D/A is scored against six factors that determine real development value - not just land value. The methodology combines spatial analysis, planning data, and market intelligence. This page explains what we measure and what gets flagged.

Intelligence Engine

The six factors

Each factor is scored 0-100 and weighted by its influence on development viability. Relative weights are proprietary - calibrated against a reference set of City of London and SE1 consented schemes.

Available Sky
Height headroom relative to the surrounding cluster. Sites deep within a tall buildings zone with underbuilt neighbours score highest. Derived from contextual building height analysis within 300m.
Rent Headroom
Gap between current passing rent and achievable ERV for the proposed use. Measures the value-creation opportunity from intensification. Calibrated to borough-level prime ERV data.
Planning Tailwind
How strongly local policy supports the proposed use and scale. Tall buildings zones, Opportunity Areas, and CAZ designations are positive signals. Conservation areas and Article 4 directions reduce this score.
Heritage Shadow
Proximity and weight of designated heritage assets - listed buildings, World Heritage Sites, conservation areas. Scored inversely: a high score means low heritage constraint. Grade I listings and WHS settings are heaviest penalties.
Title Stack
Complexity of the ownership and leasehold structure. Single freehold with no long leaseholds scores highest. Multi-title campus assemblies, ground leases, and highway titles all reduce this score.
Transport Links
PTAL score and walking distance to nearest Elizabeth line, Underground, or Overground station. Given the density of Central London stations, this rarely determines viability but distinguishes fringe sites.
Score Interpretation

What the number means

The composite score runs 0-100. In practice, genuinely viable development sites in Central London score 65-92. Scores above 85 are rare and typically indicate exceptional uplift potential or a structurally undervalued site.

Site Constraints

What gets flagged - and why we still show it

D/A does not remove constrained sites from results. Development context changes: leases expire, consents lapse, operators relocate, owners change their minds. A site that scores poorly today may be the most interesting opportunity in the market tomorrow. We flag constraints transparently so you can make that judgement yourself.

🚫
Active planning consent
A live permission exists. The development case is effectively already made - but consents lapse, schemes are revised, and sites do come back to market.
Heavy penalty
🏗
Under construction
Site is actively being built out. Shown for market context only.
Heavy penalty
🏬
Operational anchor tenant
Supermarket, shopping centre, or large-format retail with institutional lease in place. Possession is complex and time-consuming. EUV is elevated, suppressing residual land value.
Heavy penalty
🏛
Listed building (Grade I or II*)
The primary structure is listed. Demolition is effectively ruled out. Retention and adaptation may still be viable.
Heavy penalty
🏢
Owner-occupier institutional
The owner uses the building operationally (bank HQ, NHS in use, government office). No income-driven disposal motivation. Patience required.
Moderate penalty
⚠️
Recently completed (post-2015)
Building was constructed or comprehensively refurbished within the last decade. Development cycle is unlikely to have restarted.
Moderate penalty
🌊
Flood Zone 3
High probability of flooding. Sequential and exception tests required. Residential use is very difficult to justify.
Moderate penalty
📋
Multiple freeholders
Site assembly required across separately-owned titles. Time, cost, and negotiation risk are elevated. CPO route rarely viable for private developers.
Moderate penalty

Our position on constrained sites

The public data available to D/A - HMLR title records, EPC certificates, planning portals, OS building data - does not capture everything relevant to a site's viability. A developer with a relationship with the landowner, knowledge of an upcoming lease event, or specialist planning intelligence may see very different prospects for a flagged site.

If you have a site in mind that D/A flags as constrained, or if you want deeper analysis than the public dataset supports, get in touch. The most interesting opportunities in London are often the ones that look difficult on the surface.

Data Sources

Where the data comes from

D/A uses public and commercial datasets. No proprietary data is fabricated or estimated without disclosure.

📐
Site boundaries + ownership
HMLR (HM Land Registry) title polygons and register data. Single-title sites use the registered polygon directly. Multi-title campus sites use a convex hull of constituent titles.
HMLR
🏗
Building height + footprint
Ordnance Survey National Geographic Database (OS NGD) building parts collection. Heights are OS-surveyed where available; estimated from floor count where not.
OS NGD
⚡
Existing GIA + floor areas
EPC (Energy Performance Certificate) data from MHCLG Open Data Communities. Total certified GIA is used as the canonical existing floor area. Per-floor figures are indicative only.
EPC / MHCLG
💷
Market rents + yields
Borough-level prime ERV and NIY calibrated against published CoStar, Savills, and CBRE research. Updated periodically. D/A does not have a live market data feed - figures are research-based estimates.
Research
🗺
Planning policy + designations
London Plan, borough Local Plans, and GLA planning data. Heritage designations from Historic England's National Heritage List. Conservation area boundaries from borough GIS portals.
GLA / HE
🚇
PTAL / transport access
TfL Public Transport Accessibility Levels, derived from station walking distances and service frequency data.
TfL
